# Manuscript Rhetoric vs Evidence

Use when the manuscript is polished, journal-styled, persuasive, or when the user
asks about Nature/Cell/Science fit, writing quality, or reviewer response
strategy.

## Split the judgment

Evaluate these dimensions separately:

| Dimension | Question |
|---|---|
| Rhetorical quality | Is the story clear, coherent, and accessible? |
| Claim precision | Are claims bounded to what was actually shown? |
| Evidence sufficiency | Are the decisive controls, analyses, proofs, benchmarks, or cohorts present? |
| Causal strength | Does the evidence justify causal, mechanistic, clinical, theoretical, or benchmark-superiority language? |
| Reproducibility | Are data, code, methods, materials, uncertainty, and exclusions sufficient for verification? |
| Journal fit | Does the work meet venue novelty and rigor thresholds after evidence, not style, is accounted for? |

## Rules

- Polished prose is not evidence.
- A strong narrative can increase overclaiming risk when the decisive readout is
  missing.
- Do not reward "Nature-style" language unless it improves claim precision,
  interpretability, and reproducibility.
- Wording improvements are Minor unless wording causes a materially unsupported
  conclusion.
- If the manuscript would remain unsupported after perfect copyediting, the
  recommendation must be driven by evidence, not presentation.
